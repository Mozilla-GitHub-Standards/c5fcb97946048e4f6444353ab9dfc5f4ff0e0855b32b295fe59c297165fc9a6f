The Rules

Technology

All games must be built using Open Web technologies. This includes but is not limited to HTML, CSS, JavaScript as well as server-side code such as PHP, Python, Ruby or Java. You can use libraries (no worries, you don’t need to reinvent the wheel). No browser plug-ins are allowed (and we’re strict on this one!).

Timing

submission period starts September 29th, 2010

submissions close January 11th, 2011

This gives you a little over 3 months and an entire holiday season to develop your Open Web game during Game On 2010.

The competition kicks off at 12:00am / 00:00 GMT on September 29th, 2010 and will end at 11:00am / 11:00 GMT on January 11th, 2011. You are welcome to work on the concept for your game before the competition starts: napkin doodles, insomniac wire-framing, robots in disguise even some hacking if you like.

After time expires, an expert panel and the public will be invited to play your game and judge it against the competition. No additional features, fixes, or modifications are allowed during the judging period, or you will be disqualified. We really want to play your game, so please don’t make us disqualify you before we have the chance.

Teams and Contestants

Individuals as well as teams are welcome to participate. To be eligible, individuals and teams must submit their entry before the official competition deadline. Don’t be too late – as much as we all thrive on getting the finishing touches done in the 11th hour, we don’t want you to miss the deadline.

If you work for a sponsor – sorry, but we can’t accept your submission to this competition. And don’t worry if you know a judge: We’ll prevent judges from voting on a team they know personally and make sure they commit to being fair and objective in their evaluation.

Open Web Games

Entries should be fully operational games, built to run in Firefox 4 and other modern Web browsers without any plug-ins.

Libraries and modules that are public and freely available are allowed and encouraged. Please list all libraries that you use on your team’s profile page to give fair attribution. Stock photos and free icon sets are allowed. Paying for something that is not generally available to everyone is not. And please make sure you don’t violate the copyright of others – that Lady Gaga song might be perfect for your game, but unless you have it in writing from Gaga’s management, don’t use it.

Web Services

Feel free to make use of third-party web services and their APIs (e.g. Twitter, Flickr, Google maps, Yahoo! pipes, OpenID). Almost everyone loves mashups, and it’s fun to see what you can do with them in the context of a game. Just make sure to update your team profile listing the services you use.

Deployment

It’s completely up to you where you deploy your code. You can use your own server, public webspace you have somewhere, cloud hosting services such as Google’s App Engine or simply contact us to get access to a free, private instance of AMAZONS EC2 cloud service. Whatever you decide to do – make sure your server is up and running during the judging period. It would be a shame if we couldn’t judge your awesome game only because someone poured coffee over your the server you host behind your fridge. Entries have to remain up until winners are announced.

Ownership and Open Source

The code you write is yours. If you intend to share it after the competition, you can pick whatever license you prefer. We encourage contestants to open source their code, but that’s entirely up to you. If you decide to start a business based on it, we wish you much success.
